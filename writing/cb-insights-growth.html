Growth Case Study 02 · CB Insights

Sustainable growth for a flagship product.

How we lifted trial conversion by 35%, moved WAU from 59% to 75%, and pushed collaboration up 27% on the investment tools at CB Insights.

Naama Mendes Jr.
Senior Product Manager · CB Insights · 2022 – 2023
Issue 02

CB Insights is a primary data platform for VCs and CVCs — it leverages market and company data to help them make investment decisions. When I joined, the flagship investment tools were widely respected and quietly stalling. Trial users were churning before they reached "aha." Power users weren't returning between sessions. And collaboration — the moat — wasn't the moat we said it was.

We didn't rebuild the product. We rebuilt the funnel through it.

The three levers

One product, one quarter, three levers — each owned by a different cross-functional pod, all measured against the same north-star: are users coming back, and are they bringing their teammates.

01 · Trial

Increasing conversion of the trial experience.

Redesigned the trial flow end-to-end, leading to a 35% increase in conversion rates.

02 · WAU

Bringing users back with alerts and email.

Built an alerting and email system that lifted WAU from 59% to 75% of monthly users.

03 · Collab

Rethinking collaborative tools.

Redesigned workflow tools — investor CRM and Feeds — driving a 27% increase in collaboration rate within six months.

Steps to rebuild the trial

The trial was where the funnel hemorrhaged. Three moves, in order, made the difference.

Method

How we attacked it.

  1. Analyze every aspect of the funnel. We mapped every step and process — from sign-up touch to first saved report — to find where the opportunities actually were, not where we assumed they were.
  2. Iterate quickly. We attacked opportunities fast and filtered hypotheses through creative, dynamic experiments and a disciplined A/B testing program.
  3. Build a multidisciplinary team. I led a team of 8 across sales, marketing, and product. The pod owned the whole loop, not three teams handing off.

How the trial actually moved

Inside the rebuild, three principles did most of the work — and they're the ones I'd take to any growth-driven product team tomorrow.

Three principles, in order.

Personalize the experience. We used company data and pre-set onboarding templates per persona — the content surfaced to a corporate-development trial user wasn't the same content surfaced to a venture analyst.

Lower time to value. We stopped showing users how to use the platform and started showing them why. A guided tour through real research, ending with the user creating something similar themselves.

Prioritize buyers. We sharpened the value proposition and named the high-potential accounts up-front — then chose, deliberately, which trial users to invest in.

What I took from it

What I learned

Growth is a revenue-operations problem dressed up as a product one.

I learned how to properly include revenue-operations teams inside a growth effort — not as the audience for a quarterly readout, but as the co-designers of the funnel itself. Sales, marketing, and product had different incentives; the work was getting them to share one definition of "qualified."

I learned how to maximize the accuracy of A/B tests inside a low-volume, high-ACV product, where every cohort is small and every conclusion is fragile. And I learned how to make alerts — notifications and emails — a reliable source of user activation rather than a vanity channel.

I also got fluent in sales frameworks and the data underneath them: Salesforce, Drift, Outreach. The growth work I shipped was as much about reading those pipelines as it was about reading the product analytics.

What I'd do differently

Set the funnel's targets before the experiments.

I'd iterate more heavily on UX and sales strategy in parallel — we treated them as sequential too often, and each one waited on the other longer than it should have.

I'd set specific growth objectives for each step within the customer acquisition funnel — not a single north-star, but a target per stage, owned by a specific pair of people. The work that didn't ship is the work that didn't have a name on it.

The CB Insights chapter taught me how to grow a high-ACV product without breaking what made it loved. I carry the funnel discipline from that team into every roadmap I help write now.
