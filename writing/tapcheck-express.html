Embedded fintech Case Study 01 · Tapcheck

Distribution as product.

How I led Tapcheck Express — an embedded earned-wage-access platform that turned every payroll, HR, and time-and-attendance provider into a Tapcheck distribution channel.

Naama Mendes Jr.
Lead Product Manager · Tapcheck · 2023 – Current
Issue 01

Tapcheck is a leading earned-wage-access provider. We let employees of companies like Amazon, Home Instead, and Metro by T-Mobile take an advance on wages they've already earned, weeks before payday. When I joined, the product was loved. The way it reached people wasn't.

The whole company was built around a direct-to-employer motion. Every new customer meant convincing an HR leader, integrating with their payroll system, and standing up the program one rooftop at a time. The product was instant. Distribution was anything but.

This is the story of Tapcheck Express — the embedded platform we shipped to fix that — and what I learned treating distribution as a product surface, not a sales motion.

Tapcheck Express

Earned wage access — built for workforce platforms.

Designed for payroll, HR, and time-and-attendance providers, Tapcheck Express unlocks on-demand pay and financial wellness for their clients — instantly. Flip-the-switch simplicity. No engineering work on the partner's side. Just instant value to every business on the platform.

The distribution problem

Earned wage access isn't a product employees go and find. It arrives, or it doesn't, through the company they already work for. Which means EWA isn't really an employer-benefits product — it's a distribution problem dressed up as one.

Most of our growth was gated by a long, expensive direct-to-employer cycle: pitch HR, integrate the payroll system, train managers, launch. Meanwhile every payroll, HR, and time-and-attendance provider in the country already had thousands of employer relationships, and a product surface their clients used every week. They didn't have EWA. We didn't have their reach.

Tapcheck Express is the answer. We rebuilt our platform so any workforce-tech provider could embed Tapcheck inside their own product — under their brand or ours — with no engineering lift on their side. Their distribution. Our product. One integration.

Impact as Lead PM

The Express bet asked the platform to do things it had never done — serve multiple partners, run async of any one app, and stand up new integrations in a day instead of two weeks.

01 · Revenue

$0 to $2.2M ARR in under a quarter.

Express went from a slide deck to a live, revenue-generating platform faster than any product line in Tapcheck's history. The pipeline that had been sitting behind direct-to-employer sales unblocked the moment partners could enable us themselves.

02 · Activation

+15% registration success via SSO.

Embedding Tapcheck inside the partner's existing identity flow removed the friction layer that used to lose us employees at first contact. Users land already authenticated — fewer drop-offs, more eligible accounts.

03 · Throughput

+7% transfers from the ML earnings model.

The earnings prediction engine lifted transfers across the eligible base — and removed the time-clock dependency that used to gate every new partner integration.

How Express works

The product surface is small on purpose. A workforce-tech partner connects once. Every employer on their platform inherits Tapcheck. Every employee at those employers can transfer earned wages from inside the partner's app, the Tapcheck app, or a text message.

What Express promises a partner

Express is sold to platform partners on six commitments. Each one is a product decision — not a marketing line — and each one shaped a chunk of the platform we shipped.

01

Zero technical lift.

Tapcheck handles setup, integration, and ongoing support. No engineering work on the partner side.

02

Flexible by design.

Connect payroll, time-and-attendance, or both — pick the integration model that fits the partner's platform.

03

No change to payroll.

Existing payroll cycles and processes stay untouched. Express runs alongside them, not through them.

04

Seamless for the employee.

Wages, pay summaries, and financial education in one experience — embedded in the partner app or in Tapcheck's.

05

Free for the business.

No cost to employers. Partners get a differentiator their competitors don't have, with no line on their clients' invoice.

06

Enterprise-grade by default.

Built to the highest standards of data security and regulatory compliance — trusted by platforms that serve millions of businesses.

The platform work behind it

The promises above only work if the platform underneath earns them. Three pieces of platform work made Express ship.

01 Async services

Externalized balance generation and transfer calls.

Moved off the critical path of the consumer app so any partner can call them async without bottlenecking each other. The same balance call now serves the Tapcheck app, an embedded experience inside Workday, and a text-to-transfer flow — without queuing.

02 Earnings ML

An AI engine that predicts a minimal gross balance.

Reduces the need for a time-clock integration — which used to gate every new partner. Most users now reach an eligible balance without one, which means a partner can ship Express with payroll alone, then add time-and-attendance later as an optimization.

03 Real-time data

Individualized partner calls for the integrations that do need them.

Speeds the delivery of new data to the front-end systems that show balances — so the user-visible numbers are right, inside whatever surface the partner exposes. The work that nobody outside the team saw, and the work that made every other claim above defensible.

What I took from Express

What I learned

Distribution belongs in the product, not next to it.

The biggest lever in EWA isn't the transfer experience — it's where the transfer is offered. Once we accepted that, "ship Express" stopped being a platform initiative and became the strategy.

I learned how to think through a problem holistically — distribution, platform, and pricing as one system rather than three roadmaps. Externalizing balance generation only mattered because it unlocked a partner integration; the partner integration only mattered because it bypassed the direct-to-employer cycle. Each piece earned its place by what it unlocked elsewhere.

I learned how to prepare a platform for scale before the scale arrives — not after. The integrations work that took us from 16 days to 1 was scoped a quarter before the partner pipeline that needed it.

What I'd do differently

Make the success metric visible to the team that ships it.

I'd drive harder toward clear, easily accessible OKRs — visible to the non-technical folks who built around us. The teams that didn't see the active-user number on a dashboard were the teams whose work drifted off-strategy first.

I'd further differentiate communication across teams — find the buy-in story for sales, for engineering, for partner ops separately, with a focus on each team's specific job-to-be-done. One narrative is cheap to write and expensive to ship.

Express is the chapter I'm most proud of from Tapcheck. It taught me that the most strategic surface a fintech product owns isn't the screen the user taps — it's the ability to be where the user already is. Meet them inside the partner app, the payroll portal, the text thread they're already reading. Distribution stops being a sales motion and starts being a product question.
